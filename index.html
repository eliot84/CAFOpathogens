The U.S. CAFO

Concentrated Animal Feeding Operations (CAFO), is a term coined by the US Environmental. A CAFO is a facility that raises animals at high-densities for the consumption of meat, eggs and milk. There are rewarding incentives in the current agricultural system to cultivate larger farms with lower costs that result in greater profits. This article does not focus on those rewards; instead, we wish to bring to light concern regarding the practices of these CAFO locations. Specifically, how they have the ability to impact the environment and human health through increased pathogenic cultivation.

Waste management in large venues like these are concerning not only in situations where the amount of manure produced exceeds the demand for manure. Circumstances where water contamination and live stock become carriers of pathogens can become very difficult to resolve.

Foodborne Outbreaks 1998 - 2008 by State

Manure is valuable to the farming industry, but at large production quantities they become problematic. The Environmental Protection Agency has classified the single most pressing public health issue associated with CAFO locations specifically being the amount of manure they produce. Although manure contain plant nutrients such as nitrogen and phosphorus, they also contain a variety of potential contaminants including bacterial and viral pathogens. Although sewage treatment plants are required for human waste, no such treatment facility exists for livestock waste at these CAFO locations.
